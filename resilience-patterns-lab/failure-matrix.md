## Mini-lab 3: Circuit breaker

**Observable claim:** After the failure threshold trips, the client fails fast without contacting upstream — upstream request count flatlines — and half-open probes, not the timer alone, decide recovery.

| # | Scenario | Prediction (verbatim) | Observed | Verdict | Takeaway |
|---|----------|------------------------|----------|---------|----------|
| 1 | latencyMs=20, errorRate=0.6 (sustained, never improves), concurrencyCap=1000 (no capacity constraint), timeout=200, retries=0, breaker on: threshold=0.5, window=20, openMs=5000, halfOpenProbes=1, rate=20/s, 25s | "1. attempts that actually reach upstream are serviced and pass/fail based on .6 factor 2. it reopens, closes, reopens, the cycle goes on" | Breaker tripped OPEN by t=2 (window filled, ~60% failed). `sent` (client-side) dropped to exactly 0 for the full duration of every OPEN window, confirmed independently via `upstream_requests_total{outcome="received"}` in Prometheus — `rate()` on that series also went to exactly 0.0 req/s during those same windows (client and upstream metrics agree to the second). Breaker did **not** stay permanently open: two full cycles observed in 25s — OPEN (~5s, matches `breakerOpenMs`) → single half-open probe → probe succeeded (~40% chance, since errorRate=0.6 means P(success)=0.4) → CLOSED for ~1s (window refilling) → re-tripped OPEN once the fresh window hit >50% failures again. Other probe attempts (t=13, t=24) failed and sent it straight back to OPEN without ever closing. | ✓ correct, both parts | Flatline claim confirmed from both client and server vantage points, not just self-reported client stats. The oscillation prediction was the more interesting call: since `breakerGate()` only checks `state`, a CLOSED breaker passes every request with no gating at all — the rolling window is purely bookkeeping until it fills. So each time a half-open probe happens to succeed, the breaker fully re-opens the gate, and with a sustained (not permanent) upstream failure rate, it takes only `window/rate` seconds (~1s here) for the fresh window to re-cross the trip threshold. The 5s `breakerOpenMs` timer only controls *when a probe is attempted* — the coin-flip outcome of that one probe (≈ 1−errorRate chance of success) is what actually decides CLOSED-vs-OPEN each cycle. A breaker facing a partial (not total) sustained failure rate doesn't settle into a fixed state; it settles into a metastable oscillation gated by probe luck. |
| 2 | Same as #1 but errorRate=1.0 (permanent, total failure) — isolates whether the timer alone can ever recover the breaker | "it stays shut off (stuck OPEN), never recovering, endlessly re-probing and reopening" (clarified via follow-up: "closed" meant closed-for-business, not the technical CLOSED state) | Tripped OPEN at t=2 and **never once reached CLOSED** for the rest of the 25s run. Exactly one half-open probe fired every 5s on the dot — `sent=1` at t=2, 7, 12, 17, 22 (perfectly matching `breakerOpenMs`) — each one failed (P(success)=0 at errorRate=1.0) and the state immediately snapped back to OPEN with `breakerRejected` resuming at ~19-20/s. 5 probe attempts, 5 failures, 0 closes. | ✓ correct | Directly isolates the timer from the probe: the 5s clock reliably re-arms a probe attempt like clockwork, but arming a probe is not the same as recovering — recovery is gated entirely by that probe's real outcome. With a upstream that never improves, the breaker is stable in a *permanently* re-tripping OPEN state, in clean contrast to scenario 1's oscillation — the only variable that changed between the two scenarios was whether a probe *could* succeed (errorRate 0.6 vs 1.0), and that alone was the difference between "metastable oscillation" and "permanently shut". Confirms the brief's claim precisely: it's the half-open probe, not the timer, that decides recovery — the timer only decides opportunity. |
| 3 | Same params as #1 (errorRate=0.6, oscillating breaker), but upstream is live-patched to errorRate=0 at t=15s via `/admin/config` (no restart), duration=30s — tests genuine recovery detection, not probe luck | "1. the breaker closes and stays closed for the rest of the run 2. still bounded by the 5s probe [cadence]" | Ran identically to scenario 1 for the first 14s (trip → probe-fails-reopens → probe-succeeds-closes-briefly → re-trips pattern, breaker OPEN again as of t=14). errorRate flipped to 0 at t=15, but the breaker was mid-OPEN-window at that instant (last tripped at t=14) and stayed OPEN through t=18 — the fix landed with ~4s still left on the already-armed 5s timer. Next probe fired at t=19 (exactly 5s after the t=14 trip), succeeded (upstream now genuinely healthy), and the breaker went CLOSED and **stayed CLOSED for all 11 remaining seconds** (t=19/20–30): `fail=0` every second, `breakerRejected=0` every second, no re-trip. | ✓ correct, both parts | Once probes stop failing, there's nothing left to re-trip the window on — scenario 1's oscillation was entirely driven by the sustained *residual* failure rate refilling the window past threshold; remove that (errorRate=0) and the window simply never crosses 50% again, so CLOSED is a true stable fixed point here, not another lucky interval. The recovery-latency finding is the sharper one: the breaker didn't notice the fix at t=15, it noticed at t=19 — detection latency was however much of the *already in-progress* OPEN timer happened to be left when the real-world fix landed, not a fixed delay measured from the fix itself. In the worst case (fix lands the instant a fresh 5s OPEN window starts), a real recovery could sit undetected for a full `breakerOpenMs` before the client even gets a chance to notice — the timer bounds probe *opportunity*, and that bound applies just as much to genuine recoveries as to lucky ones. |
| 4 (user-run) | Same params as #1 (errorRate=0.6, threshold=0.5, window=20, openMs=5000, rate=20/s, timeout=200, retries=0) but breakerHalfOpenProbes=5 instead of 1, duration=30s — tests whether more concurrent half-open probes changes recovery odds | "1. around ~5 2. lt closes less often" (predicting `sent`≈5 during each half-open transition second, and fewer closes than the halfOpenProbes=1 baseline in scenario 1) | 5 half-open decision points across the run (t=7, 13, 19, 24, 30): **4 succeeded, 1 failed** (80% close rate) vs. scenario 1's 2-of-4 (50%) at halfOpenProbes=1 — closed *more* often, not less. `sent` during transition seconds was never near 5: seconds that ended up CLOSED showed sent=19,18,15,14 (near-full rate); the one that ended up OPEN showed sent=1. | ✗ both parts (opposite of predicted direction on #2; #1's mechanism was right in principle but the observed numbers didn't land near 5 either way) | `recordBreakerResult` flips state on the **first** result to arrive during HALF_OPEN, not a vote across all in-flight probes — so `halfOpenProbes=5` only matters if requests genuinely overlap in flight. At rate=20/s the load generator dispatches every 50ms, but upstream latency is ~20-23ms, so the first probe always resolves before the second is even dispatched — the "5 concurrent probes" ceiling is never exercised, and the setting collapses to behaving exactly like halfOpenProbes=1 (same ~40% per-decision success probability). The 80%-vs-50% gap is small-sample variance (n=5 and n=4) around that same true rate, not a systematic effect of the higher probe count. Explains the `sent` numbers too: once the single winning probe decides CLOSED, every other request that same 1s tick (there are ~20 of them) passes through unfiltered under the now-CLOSED gate — hence sent jumping to 15-19, not clustering near 5; a losing probe flips back to OPEN just as fast, rejecting everything else that tick — hence sent=1. **Takeaway: `halfOpenProbes` is a config knob whose real-world effect is silently gated by an unrelated variable (load-generator cadence vs. upstream latency) that its name gives no hint of — raising it only changes behavior once request rate is fast enough, relative to upstream latency, for probes to actually overlap in flight.** |

## Mini-lab 4: Load shedding

**Observable claim:** Does early rejection bound p99 for accepted work vs. queuing degrading everything?

| # | Scenario | Prediction (verbatim) | Observed | Verdict | Takeaway |
|---|----------|------------------------|----------|---------|----------|

Queuing makes every accepted request wait behind an ever-growing line (unbounded, linear p99 growth, no data loss but unusable latency within seconds), while shedding rejects the excess in ~1-9ms (before it ever touches the slow path) and leaves accepted-work latency pinned at the base processing time indefinitely. | 2 | Same overload setup as #1(b) (shedMode=shed, latencyMs=200, concurrencyCap=5, rate=40/s, timeout=0, duration=20s) but with retries=3, backoff=none added — shed responses get retried exactly like fail/timeout | "1. sent stays flat, extra load is shed 2. shed grows along with sent 3. p99 stays pinned near 200ms 4. success stays around 25/s" | `sent` jumped immediately at t=1 to ~81-89/s — roughly **2x** scenario 1(b)'s ~40/s baseline — then stayed flat at that new elevated level for the full 20s (no escalation over time, no storm). `shed` likewise jumped immediately to ~56-64/s (~4x scenario 1(b)'s ~15/s) and also stayed flat, tracking `sent` in lockstep the whole run. `success` stayed at 24-25/s throughout, identical to the no-retry baseline. p99 stayed pinned at 202-209ms the whole run, statistically indistinguishable from scenario 1(b)'s 201-210ms — but p50 collapsed from ~200ms (scenario 1(b)) to **1ms** here, since the majority of individual attempts are now near-instant sheds rather than 200ms successes. | (3) ✓ exact / (4) ✓ exact / (1)+(2) mostly ✓ on the qualitative claim (no runaway storm, stable from t=1 — a real distinction from mini-lab 2's retries-against-a-slow-upstream storm) but imprecise on magnitude: "flat" undersold that sent/shed jumped to a *new, ~2x/~4x higher* plateau immediately, not "flat at the same level as before" | No storm here, unlike mini-lab 2's retry-against-slow-upstream case — because shed responses resolve in ~1-9ms with zero queueing delay, there's no growing latency to cross a timeout threshold and build momentum; the system just jumps straight to a new (worse) stable equilibrium in the first second and holds it, rather than escalating over several seconds before collapsing.
